The School That Never Taught Me Anything That Actually Mattered

My name is Lucas. I'm 19, I run my own business, and none of what I know came from school. This is my story about the two ways the education system failed me and what I had to do because of it.

01 — My story

What actually happened to me

My dad passed away when I was in high school. That was the hardest thing I've ever been through, and honestly it still is. I was young, I didn't know how to process it, and nobody at my school really knew how to handle it either.

After he passed, the bullying started. I was already in a dark place and when people came at me I reacted. I got in trouble. And instead of anyone pulling me aside and asking what was going on at home, the school just kept writing me up. Detentions. Suspensions. A label that stuck to me no matter where I went. Every time something happened it made things worse, and nobody ever stopped to ask why it kept happening in the first place. They just kept punishing me like that was going to fix something.

"I was going through the worst thing of my life and the school's response was to keep writing me up."

— Lucas, MOVERE founder

That cycle followed me for years. And when I eventually tried to build something for myself, a real business online, I ran into the second way the system had failed me. I had to learn everything completely from scratch. How to use AI tools. How to market on social media. How to source products, run ads, build a brand. None of it was ever taught to me. I figured it out on my own because I had no other choice.

Two different failures. Same school system. And I know I'm not the only one this happened to.

02 — Why it matters

This is bigger than just my situation

What happened to me isn't some rare thing. Schools all over the country are doing this to students every single day. When something hard happens in your life, like losing a parent, the system isn't built to support you. It's built to manage you. And when it can't manage you, it punishes you.

At the same time, we're living in a world where knowing how to build things online is one of the most important skills you can have. You can run a business from your phone. You can reach millions of people with a video. You can make a real income without a degree. But schools aren't teaching any of that. So the students who figure it out do it on their own, and the ones who don't just get left behind.

Both of these failures hit hardest on the kids who already have the least going for them. The ones without money, without connections, without a safety net. If you're already at a disadvantage and the school punishes your grief and graduates you without real skills, you're basically starting from zero while everyone else already has a head start.

"Schools were not designed to interrupt inequality. They were designed to reproduce it."

— Jonathan Kozol, Savage Inequalities

03 — What the research says

The data backs this up

A 2022 peer reviewed systematic review published in Omega: Journal of Death and Dying looked at 22 studies on how bereavement affects students' educational outcomes. The researchers found that losing someone close is a direct barrier to academic performance, engagement, and long term educational success. Students who are grieving struggle to focus, fall behind, and disengage from school. But instead of responding to that with support, most schools respond with discipline. The study calls for targeted interventions and better trained staff to actually help grieving students instead of just flagging them as problems.

On the digital side, research published in Computers and Education Open found that students from lower income backgrounds and marginalized communities consistently have less access to real digital skills training. The schools that serve students who need the most support are also the ones least equipped to teach them how to operate in a digital economy. That gap doesn't close on its own. It compounds over time and limits what those students are able to do after they graduate.

Put those two things together and you can see the full picture. Schools are failing grieving students by punishing them, and failing all students by not preparing them for the world they're actually going to live in. The students who fall through both of those cracks end up having to figure everything out on their own, or not at all.

04 — MOVERE

What I built on my own

At 19, while still going to community college, I started MOVERE. It's a fitness accessory brand. I sell on Amazon, TikTok Shop, and my own website. I sourced my first product from a manufacturer in China, set up an LLC, opened a business bank account, and taught myself how to run paid ads, create content, and manage a supply chain. All of it from scratch. All of it self-taught.

The name means "to move" in Latin. I chose it because that's what I've had to do my whole life. My dad passing, getting in trouble in school, having nobody really show me the way. None of it stopped me but it would have been a completely different story if someone had actually stepped in when I was struggling, or if school had given me even one class on how to build something online.

"MOVERE isn't just a brand. It's proof that you can build something real even when the system gives you nothing to work with."

— Lucas

I'm not saying this to brag. I'm saying it because I know how many people are out there who went through something similar and didn't make it out the other side. The difference isn't always about how hard you work. Sometimes it's about whether anyone was paying attention when you needed them to be.

05 — What needs to change

Real solutions

🤝
Stop punishing students for grieving Schools need to train staff to actually recognize when a student is acting out because they are hurting. Responding with consequences instead of support makes everything worse and research shows it.
👥
Give grieving students a real mentor, not just a write up When a student loses a parent they need someone actually in their corner. A counselor they see once is not enough. Schools should have dedicated support systems for students going through loss.
💻
Make digital literacy and AI a required class, not an elective Every student should graduate knowing how to use AI tools, market themselves online, and operate in a digital economy. This is not optional anymore. It is a basic life skill.
📈
Teach entrepreneurship like it actually matters How to start a business, manage money, build a brand, run social media. These things change lives and schools almost never teach them. They should be standard, especially in communities that need economic mobility the most.
